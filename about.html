About Dr. Turk...

What is Dr. Turk?

Doctor Turk is a Dr. Phil-esque advice source where users can ask questions about any given topic and quickly receive quality, crowdsourced responses.

What were our sources of inspiration for project?

We modeled the concept for Dr. Turk off of popular advice columns that run on blogs or newspapers. You may be familiar with r/advice, Quora, Dear Abby, StackExchange, Yahoo! Answers, WikiAnswers, and WolframAlpha. These websites rely on users to contribute content by both asking questions and providing answers. Some people who answer the questions may be subject-matter “experts” or people who happen to just know about a topic (for example, a wizard in Python answering a specific Python syntax question on StackExchange). But the platforms mentioned above do not pay users for contributing so responders on these platforms do not necessarily have clear motivations for answering without any monetary or other incentive, besides altruism. So in many cases, the users answering questions are not in fact experts and the advice they provide neither accurate nor useful. Moreover, the responses can often be confusing and contradictory, and an array of upvotes, downvotes, and likes can stymie users. To distinguish Doctor Turk from these other forums, we proposed a minimalist interface. Users simply visit doctorturk.co.
The site allows users to learn about the project, view our analysis, and participate by submitting a question they would like to have answered.
What happens when a user submits a question?

From a user perspective, you simply click “Submit a question” on the site. The form prompts you to enter your e-mail address and ask your questions. Each form allows one question. However, there is no limit to the number of forms a single user can submit, so post away!
Under the hood, a batch Crowdflower job is generated, asking workers to supply 100-2000 character answers. After we obtain ten answers, a new job is posted, asking workers to vote. Then, these votes are aggregated, and the best-ranked one is submitted for a final check. If workers deny it then we’re back to the drawing board for ten fresh answers; otherwise it’s sent to the original querier.
What happens after questions are answered?

After an initial question from a user is answered, we post a second set of jobs where we ask the crowd to choose the answer they think is “best” of all the responses, to be presented to the original querier.
